A condensed list of every key equation across all 13 units. Scroll or search.

Kinematics

$$v=\tfrac{dx}{dt},\quad a=\tfrac{dv}{dt}=\tfrac{d^2x}{dt^2}$$ $$v=v_0+at,\ x=x_0+v_0t+\tfrac12 at^2,\ v^2=v_0^2+2a\Delta x$$

Newton's Laws

$$\sum \vec F = m\vec a = \tfrac{d\vec p}{dt}$$ $$f_s\le\mu_s N,\ f_k=\mu_k N$$

Work / Energy / Power

$$W=\int\vec F\cdot d\vec r,\ K=\tfrac12 mv^2$$ $$F_x=-\tfrac{dU}{dx},\ P=\vec F\cdot\vec v$$

Momentum

$$\vec p = m\vec v,\ \vec J=\int\vec F\,dt=\Delta\vec p$$ $$\vec r_{cm}=\frac{1}{M}\int\vec r\,dm$$

Rotation

$$\vec\tau=\vec r\times\vec F,\ \sum\vec\tau=I\vec\alpha$$ $$I=\int r^2 dm,\ I=I_{cm}+Md^2$$

Rotational Energy / Angular Momentum

$$K_{rot}=\tfrac12 I\omega^2,\ L=I\omega=\vec r\times\vec p$$ $$\tau_{ext}=\tfrac{dL}{dt}$$

Gravitation / Orbits

$$F=\tfrac{GMm}{r^2},\ U=-\tfrac{GMm}{r}$$ $$E_{orbit}=-\tfrac{GMm}{2a},\ T^2=\tfrac{4\pi^2 a^3}{GM}$$

SHM

$$\tfrac{d^2x}{dt^2}=-\omega^2 x,\ \omega=\sqrt{k/m}$$ $$T_{phys}=2\pi\sqrt{I/mgd},\ E=\tfrac12 kA^2$$

Coulomb & Gauss

$$\vec E=\tfrac{kq}{r^2}\hat r,\ \oint\vec E\cdot d\vec A=\tfrac{Q_{enc}}{\varepsilon_0}$$

Potential

$$V=\tfrac{kq}{r},\ V_B-V_A=-\int_A^B\vec E\cdot d\vec l$$ $$\vec E=-\nabla V,\ U=qV$$

Capacitance

$$C=Q/V,\ C_{pp}=\tfrac{\varepsilon_0 A}{d}$$ $$U=\tfrac12 CV^2,\ u=\tfrac12\varepsilon_0 E^2$$

Circuits / RC

$$V=IR,\ P=I^2 R$$ $$q(t)=Q_f(1-e^{-t/RC}),\ \tau=RC$$

Magnetism

$$\vec F=q\vec v\times\vec B,\ r=\tfrac{mv}{qB}$$ $$d\vec B=\tfrac{\mu_0}{4\pi}\tfrac{I\,d\vec l\times\hat r}{r^2},\ \oint\vec B\cdot d\vec l=\mu_0 I_{enc}$$

Induction / LR / LC

$$\varepsilon=-\tfrac{d\Phi_B}{dt}=-L\tfrac{dI}{dt}$$ $$U=\tfrac12 LI^2,\ \tau=L/R,\ \omega_{LC}=\tfrac{1}{\sqrt{LC}}$$
Constants: $g=9.8\,\mathrm{m/s^2}$, $k=8.99\times 10^9\,\mathrm{N\cdot m^2/C^2}$, $\varepsilon_0=8.85\times 10^{-12}\,\mathrm{C^2/(N\cdot m^2)}$, $\mu_0=4\pi\times 10^{-7}\,\mathrm{T\cdot m/A}$, $e=1.6\times 10^{-19}\,\mathrm C$, $G=6.67\times 10^{-11}\,\mathrm{N\cdot m^2/kg^2}$.
Mechanics ┬Ę Unit 1

Kinematics

Describe motion in 1D and 2D using calculus. Position, velocity, and acceleration are related by derivatives and integrals; constant-acceleration kinematics are a special case.

Learning Objectives

  • Describe motion in 1D/2D with position, displacement, velocity, acceleration.
  • Derive $v$ and $a$ from $x(t)$ by differentiation; derive $v$ and $x$ from $a(t)$ by integration.
  • Apply constant-acceleration kinematic equations.
  • Analyze projectile motion by decomposing into independent $x$ and $y$ components.
  • Interpret $x$ŌĆō$t$, $v$ŌĆō$t$, $a$ŌĆō$t$ graphs (slopes = derivatives, areas = integrals).

Key Equations

$$v(t)=\frac{dx}{dt},\quad a(t)=\frac{dv}{dt},\quad x(t)=x_0+\int_0^t v(t')\,dt'$$ $$v=v_0+at,\quad x=x_0+v_0 t+\tfrac12 at^2,\quad v^2=v_0^2+2a(x-x_0)$$ $$\text{Projectile: }v_{0x}=v_0\cos\theta,\ v_{0y}=v_0\sin\theta,\ a_y=-g$$

Diagram

Worked Examples

Example 1 Non-constant acceleration (calculus)

Problem. A particle moves along the $x$-axis with $a(t)=(6t-4)\,\mathrm{m/s^2}$. At $t=0$, $x_0=2\,\mathrm m$ and $v_0=3\,\mathrm{m/s}$. Find $v(t)$, $x(t)$, and whether $v=0$ ever occurs.

  1. $v(t)=3+\int_0^t(6t'-4)dt'=3t^2-4t+3\,\mathrm{m/s}$
  2. $x(t)=2+\int_0^t v\,dt'=t^3-2t^2+3t+2\,\mathrm m$
  3. Set $v=0$: discriminant $=16-36=-20<0$ ŌåÆ $v$ is never zero.
Example 2 2D projectile from a cliff

Problem. Ball launched from edge of a 20 m cliff at $v_0=25\,\mathrm{m/s}$, $\theta=37┬░$. Find time to land, range, and speed at impact. (sin 37┬░ = 0.60, cos 37┬░ = 0.80)

  1. Components: $v_{0x}=20$, $v_{0y}=15\,\mathrm{m/s}$.
  2. $y(t)=20+15t-4.9t^2=0 \Rightarrow t\approx \mathbf{4.07\,s}$.
  3. Range $R=v_{0x}\cdot t\approx \mathbf{81.3\,m}$.
  4. $v_y=15-9.8(4.07)\approx-24.8$; $|\vec v|=\sqrt{20^2+24.8^2}\approx \mathbf{31.9\,m/s}$.
Example 3 Graph interpretation

Problem. A car's $v$ŌĆō$t$ graph is a straight line from $(0,4)$ to $(10,24)$ m/s. Find acceleration and displacement.

  1. $a=\text{slope}=(24-4)/10=\mathbf{2.0\,m/s^2}$.
  2. $\Delta x=\text{area}=\tfrac12(4+24)(10)=\mathbf{140\,m}$.

Common Pitfalls

  1. Using $v=v_0+at$ when $a$ is not constant ŌĆö must integrate $a(t)$.
  2. Mixing $x$- and $y$-components in projectiles ŌĆö they're independent.
  3. Sign errors with $g$; at peak, $v_y=0$ but speed ŌēĀ 0 (still have $v_x$).
Mechanics ┬Ę Unit 2

Force & Translational Dynamics

Newton's laws, friction, inclined planes, tension, and drag forces ŌĆö including derivations of terminal velocity via ODEs.

Learning Objectives

  • Draw complete free-body diagrams (FBDs).
  • Apply Newton's 1st, 2nd, 3rd laws to single and connected bodies.
  • Analyze static vs kinetic friction; find impending-motion conditions.
  • Solve inclined-plane, Atwood, and pulley problems.
  • Set up and solve drag ODEs; derive terminal velocity.

Key Equations

$$\sum\vec F=m\vec a=\frac{d\vec p}{dt}$$ $$f_s\le\mu_s N,\qquad f_k=\mu_k N$$ $$\text{Incline: } mg\sin\theta\ (\text{along}),\ N=mg\cos\theta\ (\perp)$$ $$\text{Linear drag: } m\frac{dv}{dt}=mg-bv\ \Rightarrow\ v(t)=\frac{mg}{b}(1-e^{-bt/m}),\ v_{term}=\frac{mg}{b}$$

Diagram

Worked Examples

Example 1 Incline + pulley with friction

$m_1=4$ kg on 30┬░ incline ($\mu_k=0.20$); $m_2=3$ kg hanging. Find $a$ and $T$.

  1. B falls: $m_2g-T=m_2a$. A up slope: $T-m_1g\sin\theta-\mu_k m_1g\cos\theta=m_1a$.
  2. Sum: $29.4-19.6-6.79=3.01=7a$ ŌåÆ $\mathbf{a=0.43\,m/s^2}$.
  3. $T=m_2(g-a)=3(9.37)=\mathbf{28.1\,N}$.
Example 2 Linear drag, terminal velocity

$m=0.05$ kg raindrop, $b=0.02$ kg/s. Find $v(t)$, $v_{term}$, and time to reach 95% of $v_{term}$.

  1. Separable ODE ŌåÆ $v(t)=\frac{mg}{b}(1-e^{-bt/m})$.
  2. $v_{term}=0.05(9.8)/0.02=\mathbf{24.5\,m/s}$.
  3. $\tau=m/b=2.5\,\mathrm s$; $t=\tau\ln 20=\mathbf{7.49\,s}$.
Example 3 Static friction threshold

Crate sits on ramp; $\mu_s=0.40$. Max angle before slipping?

  1. $\tan\theta=\mu_s \Rightarrow \theta_{max}=\arctan(0.40)=\mathbf{21.8┬░}$.

Common Pitfalls

  1. Assuming $N=mg$ on inclines ŌĆö it's $mg\cos\theta$.
  2. Treating static friction as always $\mu_s N$ ŌĆö it's $\le\mu_s N$ until impending slip.
  3. Sign errors in drag: drag always opposes velocity.
Mechanics ┬Ę Unit 3

Work, Energy & Power

Line integrals of force, potential-energy functions, equilibria from $U(x)$ graphs, and conservation of mechanical energy with dissipation.

Learning Objectives

  • Calculate work from constant and variable forces via line integrals.
  • Apply the workŌĆōenergy theorem $W_{net}=\Delta K$.
  • Derive force from potential energy: $F_x=-dU/dx$.
  • Identify stable/unstable equilibria on $U(x)$ graphs.
  • Apply conservation of mechanical energy, including dissipation.
  • Compute instantaneous and average power.

Key Equations

$$W=\int\vec F\cdot d\vec r,\ K=\tfrac12 mv^2,\ W_{net}=\Delta K$$ $$F_x=-\frac{dU}{dx},\ U_{grav}=mgy\text{ or }-\frac{GMm}{r},\ U_{spring}=\tfrac12 kx^2$$ $$P=\frac{dW}{dt}=\vec F\cdot\vec v,\ K_i+U_i=K_f+U_f+|W_{nc}|$$

Diagram

Worked Examples

Example 1 Variable force (line integral)

$F(x)=(6x-2x^2)\,\mathrm N$ from $x=0$ to $x=3$ m.

  1. $W=\int_0^3(6x-2x^2)dx=[3x^2-\tfrac23 x^3]_0^3=27-18=\mathbf{9\,J}$.
  2. If $m=2$ kg from rest: $v_f=\sqrt{2W/m}=\mathbf{3\,m/s}$.
Example 2 Spring + friction

$k=200$ N/m, compressed $x=0.10$ m, $m=0.50$ kg, $\mu_k=0.25$. Distance block travels on rough surface?

  1. $\tfrac12 kx^2=\mu_k mg d$ ŌåÆ $1.0=1.225d$ ŌåÆ $\mathbf{d\approx 0.82\,m}$.
Example 3 U(x) equilibria & turning points

$U(x)=x^4-8x^2+10\,\mathrm J$, $m=1$ kg, total energy $E=5$ J.

  1. $dU/dx=4x(x^2-4)=0 \Rightarrow x=0,\pm 2$.
  2. $d^2U/dx^2=12x^2-16$: at $x=0$: $-16$ (unstable); at $x=\pm 2$: $+32$ (stable).
  3. Turning points: $x^4-8x^2+5=0$ ŌåÆ $x^2=4\pm\sqrt{11}$ ŌåÆ $x\approx\pm 2.72$, $\pm 0.82$ m.

Common Pitfalls

  1. Sign of $F=-dU/dx$ ŌĆö force points "downhill" on the $U$ curve.
  2. Friction work uses path length, not displacement.
  3. Work & power are dot products ŌĆö normal and centripetal forces do no work.
Mechanics ┬Ę Unit 4

Linear Momentum

Conservation, impulse, and center of mass ŌĆö in 1D and 2D, for discrete and continuous systems.

Learning Objectives

  • Define $\vec p=m\vec v$; relate to Newton II ($\vec F=d\vec p/dt$).
  • Use impulse $\vec J=\int\vec F\,dt=\Delta\vec p$ and interpret areas on $F(t)$ graphs.
  • Conserve momentum in 1D and 2D collisions (elastic, inelastic).
  • Locate center of mass for discrete/continuous distributions.
  • Apply $M\vec a_{cm}=\vec F_{ext}$; qualitative rocket/variable mass.

Key Equations

$$\vec p=m\vec v,\ \vec F_{net}=\frac{d\vec p}{dt},\ \vec J=\int\vec F\,dt=\Delta\vec p$$ $$v_f^{inelastic}=\frac{m_1 v_1+m_2 v_2}{m_1+m_2}$$ $$\vec r_{cm}=\frac{1}{M}\int\vec r\,dm,\ M\vec a_{cm}=\vec F_{ext}$$

Diagram

Worked Examples

Example 1 2D perfectly inelastic collision

Car (1500 kg, east @ 20 m/s) + truck (2500 kg, north @ 15 m/s) stick.

  1. $p_x=30\,000$, $p_y=37\,500$ kg┬Ęm/s. $M=4000$.
  2. $v_x=7.5$, $v_y=9.375$ m/s. $|v|=\mathbf{12.0\,m/s}$, $\theta=\mathbf{51.3┬░\ N\ of\ E}$.
Example 2 Impulse from F(t)

$m=0.20$ kg from rest; $F(t)=50t-10t^2$ N for $0\le t\le 5$ s.

  1. $J=\int_0^5(50t-10t^2)dt=625-\tfrac{1250}{3}=\mathbf{208.3\,N\cdot s}$.
  2. $\Delta v=J/m=\mathbf{1042\,m/s}$ (idealized force profile).
Example 3 Center of mass of non-uniform rod

$\lambda(x)=\lambda_0(1+x/L)$.

  1. $M=\tfrac32\lambda_0 L$; $\int x\,dm=\tfrac56\lambda_0 L^2$.
  2. $x_{cm}=\mathbf{\tfrac{5}{9}L}$ ŌĆö past midpoint as expected.

Common Pitfalls

  1. Momentum is a vector ŌĆö conserve $p_x$ and $p_y$ separately.
  2. Momentum conserved in all collisions; kinetic energy only in elastic.
  3. Bouncing ball: $\Delta p=m(v_f-(-v_i))=m(v_f+v_i)$.

Torque & Rotational Dynamics

Rigid-body rotation: torque, moment of inertia by integration, rolling without slipping, and static equilibrium.

Learning Objectives

  • Apply rotational kinematics; relate linear and angular quantities.
  • Compute torque as $\vec\tau=\vec r\times\vec F$.
  • Calculate $I$ for continuous bodies via integration; apply parallel-axis theorem.
  • Apply $\sum\tau=I\alpha$ and rolling constraint $v=R\omega$.
  • Solve static-equilibrium problems (╬ŻF=0, ╬ŻŽä=0).

Key Equations

$$\vec\tau=\vec r\times\vec F,\ |\tau|=rF\sin\theta$$ $$\sum\vec\tau=I\vec\alpha,\ I=\int r^2\,dm,\ I=I_{cm}+Md^2$$ $$v_{cm}=R\omega,\ a_{cm}=R\alpha\ \text{(rolling without slipping)}$$

Worked Examples

Example 1 Moment of inertia of a rod

Uniform rod mass $M$, length $L$, axis through one end.

  1. $I=\int_0^L x^2\lambda\,dx=\lambda\cdot\tfrac{L^3}{3}=\mathbf{\tfrac{1}{3}ML^2}$.
  2. Parallel-axis check: $I=\tfrac{1}{12}ML^2+M(L/2)^2=\tfrac{1}{3}ML^2$. Ō£ō
Example 2 Sphere rolling down incline

Solid sphere, $I=\tfrac25 MR^2$, angle $\theta$. Find $a$.

  1. Translation: $Mg\sin\theta-f=Ma$.
  2. Torque about CM: $fR=I\alpha=\tfrac25 MR^2(a/R)$ ŌåÆ $f=\tfrac25 Ma$.
  3. Combine: $\mathbf{a=\tfrac{5}{7}g\sin\theta}$.
Example 3 Ladder against a wall

Uniform ladder, mass $M$, angle $\theta$ with floor, frictionless wall. Min $\mu_s$ to prevent slipping?

  1. $N_f=Mg$; torque about base: $N_w(L\sin\theta)=Mg(L/2)\cos\theta$ ŌåÆ $N_w=Mg/(2\tan\theta)$.
  2. $f=N_w\le\mu_s Mg$ ŌåÆ $\mathbf{\mu_s\ge 1/(2\tan\theta)}$.

Common Pitfalls

  1. Forgetting $\sin\theta$ in torque / using the wrong lever arm.
  2. Misapplying parallel-axis (must shift from CM axis).
  3. Assuming $f=\mu N$ for rolling ŌĆö static friction is whatever's needed, $\le\mu_s N$.
Mechanics ┬Ę Unit 6

Energy & Momentum of Rotating Systems

Rotational kinetic energy, angular momentum conservation, orbital mechanics, and Kepler's laws.

Learning Objectives

  • Compute rotational kinetic energy (including rolling).
  • Apply rotational workŌĆōenergy theorem.
  • Calculate $\vec L$ for particles and rigid bodies; apply $\tau_{ext}=dL/dt$.
  • Conserve angular momentum when $\tau_{ext}=0$.
  • Analyze orbits; derive Kepler's 3rd law.

Key Equations

$$K_{rot}=\tfrac12 I\omega^2,\ K_{roll}=\tfrac12 Mv^2+\tfrac12 I_{cm}\omega^2$$ $$\vec L=I\vec\omega=\vec r\times\vec p,\ \tau_{ext}=\frac{dL}{dt}$$ $$v_{orb}=\sqrt{GM/r},\ v_{esc}=\sqrt{2GM/r},\ E_{orbit}=-\frac{GMm}{2a}$$ $$T^2=\frac{4\pi^2 a^3}{GM}\ \text{(Kepler III)}$$

Diagram

Worked Examples

Example 1 Sphere rolling from height h
  1. $Mgh=\tfrac12 Mv^2+\tfrac12\tfrac{2}{5}MR^2(v/R)^2=\tfrac{7}{10}Mv^2$.
  2. $\mathbf{v=\sqrt{10gh/7}}$ ŌĆö slower than a sliding block because rotation stores KE.
Example 2 Ball sticks to pivoted rod

Rod mass $M$, length $L$, pivoted at one end. Ball mass $m$, speed $v_0$, strikes far end perpendicularly and sticks.

  1. $L_i=mv_0 L$; $L_f=(\tfrac13 ML^2+mL^2)\omega$.
  2. $\mathbf{\omega=\dfrac{mv_0}{L(M/3+m)}}$.
Example 3 Kepler's 3rd law derivation
  1. Circular orbit: $\frac{GMm}{r^2}=\frac{mv^2}{r}$ ŌåÆ $v^2=GM/r$.
  2. $T=2\pi r/v$ ŌåÆ $\mathbf{T^2=\dfrac{4\pi^2 r^3}{GM}}$. Escape: set $E=0$ ŌåÆ $v_{esc}=\sqrt{2GM/r}$.

Common Pitfalls

  1. Treating $v$ and $\omega$ as independent for rolling ŌĆö must use $\omega=v/R$.
  2. $L=I\omega$ requires $I$ about the same axis used for $\omega$.
  3. $E_{orbit}=-GMm/(2a)$, not $-GMm/a$; and $v_{esc}=\sqrt{2}\,v_{circular}$.
Mechanics ┬Ę Unit 7

Oscillations

Simple harmonic motion: differential equation, spring-mass, pendulums (simple and physical), and energy.

Learning Objectives

  • Identify SHM by restoring force $F=-kx$.
  • Derive $\omega$, $T$, $f$ for spring-mass and pendulums.
  • Represent $x(t)$, $v(t)$, $a(t)$ as sinusoids; relate $A$, $\omega$, $\phi$.
  • Apply energy conservation $E=\tfrac12 kA^2$.
  • Recognize damped/driven oscillations qualitatively.

Key Equations

$$\frac{d^2x}{dt^2}=-\omega^2 x,\ \omega=\sqrt{k/m}$$ $$x(t)=A\cos(\omega t+\phi),\ v=-A\omega\sin(\omega t+\phi),\ a=-A\omega^2\cos(\omega t+\phi)$$ $$T_{simple}=2\pi\sqrt{L/g},\ T_{physical}=2\pi\sqrt{I/(mgd)}$$ $$E=\tfrac12 kA^2=\tfrac12 mv^2+\tfrac12 kx^2$$

Diagram

Worked Examples

Example 1 Derive SHM from Hooke's law
  1. $m\ddot x=-kx$ ŌåÆ $\ddot x=-(k/m)x$. $\omega^2=k/m$.
  2. General solution: $x(t)=A\cos(\omega t+\phi)$; $T=2\pi\sqrt{m/k}$.
Example 2 Physical pendulum (rod pivoted at end)

Rod length $L$, mass $M$; $I=\tfrac13 ML^2$, $d=L/2$.

  1. $T=2\pi\sqrt{I/(Mgd)}=2\pi\sqrt{\tfrac{ML^2/3}{MgL/2}}=\mathbf{2\pi\sqrt{2L/3g}}$.
  2. For $L=1$ m: $T\approx\mathbf{1.64\,s}$.
Example 3 Speed at x = A/2
  1. $\tfrac12 kA^2=\tfrac12 mv^2+\tfrac12 k(A/2)^2$.
  2. $v^2=(k/m)(3A^2/4)$ ŌåÆ $\mathbf{v=\tfrac{\sqrt 3}{2}\omega A\approx 0.866\,v_{max}}$.

Common Pitfalls

  1. Using $x$ instead of $A$ in $E=\tfrac12 kA^2$.
  2. Applying simple-pendulum formula to extended bodies ŌĆö use physical pendulum.
  3. Max $|a|$ at turning points, not at equilibrium (where $v$ is max).
E&M ┬Ę Unit 8

Electric Charges, Fields & Gauss's Law

Coulomb's law, electric fields from continuous distributions, flux, and Gauss's law ŌĆö the cornerstone of electrostatics.

Learning Objectives

  • Describe charging (conduction, induction, polarization).
  • Apply Coulomb's law and superposition for point charges.
  • Compute $\vec E$ for continuous distributions via integration.
  • Compute flux and apply Gauss's law with symmetry.

Key Equations

$$F=\frac{kq_1 q_2}{r^2},\ \vec E=\vec F/q,\ E_{point}=\frac{kq}{r^2}$$ $$\Phi_E=\oint\vec E\cdot d\vec A,\ \oint\vec E\cdot d\vec A=\frac{Q_{enc}}{\varepsilon_0}$$ $$E_{sheet}=\frac{\sigma}{2\varepsilon_0}\ \text{(non-conductor)},\ E_{surface}=\frac{\sigma}{\varepsilon_0}\ \text{(conductor)}$$

Diagram

Worked Examples

Example 1 Uniformly charged insulating sphere

Total charge $Q$, radius $R$.

  1. Outside ($r\ge R$): $E(4\pi r^2)=Q/\varepsilon_0$ ŌåÆ $\mathbf{E=kQ/r^2}$.
  2. Inside ($r<R$): $Q_{enc}=Q(r^3/R^3)$ ŌåÆ $\mathbf{E=kQr/R^3}$ (linear in $r$).
  3. Both expressions agree at $r=R$.
Example 2 E on axis of a charged ring

Ring radius $a$, charge $Q$, point at distance $x$ on axis.

  1. By symmetry only $x$-component survives; $\cos\theta=x/\sqrt{x^2+a^2}$.
  2. $\mathbf{E_x=\dfrac{kQx}{(x^2+a^2)^{3/2}}}$. Max at $x=a/\sqrt 2$.
Example 3 Infinite line of charge (Gauss)

Linear density $\lambda$. Use a cylindrical Gaussian surface of radius $r$, length $L$.

  1. $\Phi=E(2\pi rL)=\lambda L/\varepsilon_0$.
  2. $\mathbf{E=\dfrac{\lambda}{2\pi\varepsilon_0 r}}$, radial.

Common Pitfalls

  1. Using Gauss on non-symmetric distributions ŌĆö law always holds but isn't solvable for $E$.
  2. Confusing $Q_{enc}$ with $Q_{total}$; for insulators use volumetric fraction.
  3. Missing the $\cos\theta$ factor in continuous integrals.
E&M ┬Ę Unit 9

Electric Potential

Potential as scalar energy per charge; relating $V$ and $\vec E$ by gradient or line integral.

Learning Objectives

  • Compute $V$ from point charges and continuous distributions.
  • Relate $\vec E$ and $V$ via $\vec E=-\nabla V$ and $V=-\int\vec E\cdot d\vec l$.
  • Apply $U=qV$ and conservation of energy.
  • Compute assembly energy of discrete charge systems.

Key Equations

$$V=\frac{kq}{r},\ V=\int\frac{k\,dq}{r}$$ $$V_B-V_A=-\int_A^B\vec E\cdot d\vec l,\ E_x=-\frac{\partial V}{\partial x}$$ $$U=qV,\ U_{pair}=\frac{kq_1 q_2}{r},\ U_{assembly}=\sum_{i<j}\frac{kq_i q_j}{r_{ij}}$$

Diagram

Worked Examples

Example 1 Potential on axis of a ring

Every $dq$ is the same distance $r=\sqrt{R^2+x^2}$ from $P$.

  1. $\mathbf{V(x)=\dfrac{kQ}{\sqrt{R^2+x^2}}}$.
  2. Verify: $E_x=-dV/dx=kQx/(R^2+x^2)^{3/2}$ Ō£ō.
Example 2 Finding E from V(x,y,z)

$V=3x^2y-2yz^2$.

  1. $\vec E=-\nabla V=(-6xy,\ -3x^2+2z^2,\ 4yz)$.
  2. At $(1,2,1)$: $\vec E=(-12,-1,8)$ V/m; $|\vec E|\approx\mathbf{14.5\,V/m}$.
Example 3 Assembly energy of 3 charges

$+q$ at each corner of equilateral triangle, side $a$.

  1. $U=3\cdot kq^2/a=\mathbf{3kq^2/a}$.
  2. If one is flipped to $-q$: $U=-kq^2/a$ (bound).

Common Pitfalls

  1. $V$ is a scalar ŌĆö never decompose into components.
  2. Sign error in $V=-\int\vec E\cdot d\vec l$: moving along $\vec E$ decreases $V$.
  3. $V=kQ/r$ valid only for spherical symmetry or far-field.
E&M ┬Ę Unit 10

Conductors & Capacitors

Conductors in electrostatic equilibrium, capacitor geometries, dielectrics, energy storage, and networks.

Learning Objectives

  • Describe conductors in electrostatic equilibrium ($E=0$ inside, $V$ constant).
  • Derive $C$ for parallel-plate, spherical, cylindrical capacitors.
  • Analyze dielectrics at constant $V$ vs constant $Q$.
  • Compute stored energy and energy density.
  • Reduce series/parallel capacitor networks.

Key Equations

$$C=Q/V,\ C_{pp}=\frac{\varepsilon_0 A}{d},\ C_{sph}=\frac{4\pi\varepsilon_0 ab}{b-a}$$ $$U=\tfrac12 CV^2=\tfrac12 Q^2/C,\ u=\tfrac12\varepsilon_0 E^2$$ $$C_{dielectric}=\kappa C_0,\ \text{series: }\tfrac{1}{C_{eq}}=\sum\tfrac{1}{C_i},\ \text{parallel: }C_{eq}=\sum C_i$$

Diagram

Worked Examples

Example 1 Spherical capacitor derivation

Inner sphere radius $a$ (+Q), outer shell radius $b$ (ŌłÆQ).

  1. Gauss ($a<r<b$): $E=Q/(4\pi\varepsilon_0 r^2)$.
  2. $V_{ab}=(Q/4\pi\varepsilon_0)(1/a-1/b)$.
  3. $\mathbf{C=\dfrac{4\pi\varepsilon_0 ab}{b-a}}$.
Example 2 Capacitor network

$C_1=2$ ╬╝F and $C_2=4$ ╬╝F in series; that combo in parallel with $C_3=3$ ╬╝F across 12 V.

  1. Series: $C_{12}=(2\cdot 4)/(2+4)=4/3$ ╬╝F.
  2. Parallel total: $C_{eq}=13/3$ ╬╝F.
  3. $Q_3=36$ ╬╝C; series branch $Q=16$ ╬╝C; $V_1=8$ V, $V_2=4$ V.

  1. Series/parallel flipped vs resistors ŌĆö capacitors in series have smaller $C_{eq}$.

E&M ┬Ę Unit 11

Electric Circuits

Ohm's law, Kirchhoff's laws, multi-loop analysis, and RC circuits with first-order ODEs.

Learning Objectives

  • Define current and drift velocity; apply $R=\rho L/A$.
  • Apply Ohm's law and compute power dissipated.
  • Apply Kirchhoff's junction and loop rules.
  • Reduce resistor networks (series/parallel).
  • Solve RC charging/discharging ODEs; interpret $\tau=RC$.

Key Equations

$$I=\frac{dQ}{dt},\ V=IR,\ P=IV=I^2R=\frac{V^2}{R}$$ $$\sum I_{in}=\sum I_{out},\ \sum\Delta V_{loop}=0$$ $$q_{charge}(t)=Q_f(1-e^{-t/RC}),\ q_{discharge}(t)=Q_0 e^{-t/RC}$$

Diagram

Worked Examples

Example 1 Two-loop Kirchhoff

$\varepsilon_1=12$ V, $\varepsilon_2=6$ V, $R_1=2$, $R_2=4$, $R_3=3$ ╬®. Junction $I_1=I_2+I_3$; left loop $12=2I_1+4I_2$; right loop $6=4I_2+3I_3$.

  1. Substitute $I_1$: $12=6I_2+2I_3$ ŌåÆ $6=3I_2+I_3$.
  2. Combine with $6=4I_2+3I_3$: $12=5I_2$ ŌåÆ $\mathbf{I_2=2.4\,A}$.
  3. $\mathbf{I_3=-1.2\,A}$ (reversed direction), $\mathbf{I_1=1.2\,A}$.
Example 2 RC charging derivation

Loop: $\varepsilon-iR-q/C=0$, $i=dq/dt$.

  1. Separable ODE: $\dfrac{dq}{C\varepsilon-q}=\dfrac{dt}{RC}$.
  2. With $q(0)=0$: $\mathbf{q(t)=C\varepsilon(1-e^{-t/RC})}$, $i(t)=(\varepsilon/R)e^{-t/RC}$.
  3. At $t=\tau$: $q=0.632\,C\varepsilon$.
Example 3 RC discharging

$Q_0=10$ ╬╝C, $R=1000$ ╬®, $C=2$ ╬╝F.

  1. $q=Q_0/2$ at $t=RC\ln 2=(2\,\mathrm{ms})(0.693)\approx \mathbf{1.39\,ms}$.
  2. Total dissipated: $U=Q_0^2/(2C)=\mathbf{25\,\mu J}$.

Common Pitfalls

  1. Sign errors in Kirchhoff loops ŌĆö pick a direction and stick with it.
  2. Capacitor at $t=0^+$ acts like a wire; at steady state, an open.
  3. $\tau=RC$ uses the Th├®venin-equivalent $R$ seen by the capacitor.
E&M ┬Ę Unit 12

Magnetic Fields & Electromagnetism

Lorentz force, circular/helical motion, BiotŌĆōSavart law, and Amp├©re's law.

Learning Objectives

  • Compute $\vec F=q\vec v\times\vec B$ and force on current-carrying wires.
  • Analyze circular and helical motion in uniform $\vec B$.
  • Apply BiotŌĆōSavart law to finite wires, arcs, loops.
  • Apply Amp├©re's law to symmetric geometries (wire, coaxial, solenoid, toroid).

Key Equations

$$\vec F=q\vec v\times\vec B,\ \vec F=I\vec L\times\vec B$$ $$r=\frac{mv}{qB},\ T=\frac{2\pi m}{qB}$$ $$d\vec B=\frac{\mu_0}{4\pi}\frac{I\,d\vec l\times\hat r}{r^2},\ B_{wire}=\frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi r}$$ $$\oint\vec B\cdot d\vec l=\mu_0 I_{enc},\ B_{solenoid}=\mu_0 n I$$

Diagram

Worked Examples

Example 1 Coaxial cable (Amp├©re)

Inner solid wire radius $a$ carries $I_0$; outer shell (radius $b$) carries $-I_0$.

  1. $r<a$: $B=\mu_0 I_0 r/(2\pi a^2)$.
  2. $a<r<b$: $B=\mu_0 I_0/(2\pi r)$.
  3. $r>b$: $B=0$ (self-shielding).
Example 2 B at center of circular arc

Arc radius $R$, angle $\theta$, current $I$.

  1. $dB=(\mu_0 I\,dL)/(4\pi R^2)$; $L=R\theta$.
  2. $\mathbf{B=\dfrac{\mu_0 I\theta}{4\pi R}}$. Full loop ($\theta=2\pi$) gives $\mu_0 I/(2R)$ Ō£ō.
Example 3 Helical motion

Proton in $\vec B=0.5\,\hat z$ T with $\vec v=(3,0,4)\times 10^6$ m/s.

  1. Only $v_\perp=3\times 10^6$ contributes to circular motion.
  2. $r=mv_\perp/(qB)\approx\mathbf{6.3\,cm}$.
  3. $T=2\pi m/(qB)\approx\mathbf{1.31\times 10^{-7}\,s}$; pitch $=v_\| T\approx\mathbf{0.52\,m}$.

Common Pitfalls

  1. Dropping the $\sin\theta$ in cross product; reverse RHR for negative charges.
  2. Amp├©re only works with symmetry (use BiotŌĆōSavart for finite shapes).
  3. $T$ is speed-independent; pitch uses $v_\|$, radius uses $v_\perp$.

Electromagnetic Induction

Faraday's law, Lenz's law, motional EMF, inductance, LR circuits, and LC oscillations.

Learning Objectives

  • Compute flux $\Phi_B=\int\vec B\cdot d\vec A$.
  • Apply Faraday's and Lenz's laws.
  • Analyze motional EMF $\varepsilon=BLv$.
  • Apply $\varepsilon=-L\,dI/dt$, $U=\tfrac12 LI^2$, $u=B^2/(2\mu_0)$.
  • Solve LR circuit ODEs; recognize LC oscillation.

Key Equations

$$\Phi_B=\int\vec B\cdot d\vec A,\ \varepsilon=-\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt},\ \varepsilon_{motional}=BLv$$ $$\varepsilon_L=-L\frac{dI}{dt},\ U=\tfrac12 LI^2,\ u=\frac{B^2}{2\mu_0}$$ $$I_{growth}(t)=\frac{\varepsilon}{R}(1-e^{-t/\tau}),\ \tau=L/R,\ \omega_{LC}=\frac{1}{\sqrt{LC}}$$

Diagram

Worked Examples

Example 1 Sliding rod on rails

$L=0.50$ m, $v=4.0$ m/s, $B=0.20$ T (into page), $R=2.0$ ╬®.

  1. $\varepsilon=BLv=\mathbf{0.40\,V}$; $I=\varepsilon/R=\mathbf{0.20\,A}$.
  2. $F_{mag}=BIL=\mathbf{0.020\,N}$ (opposes motion).
  3. $P=I^2R=\mathbf{0.080\,W}=Fv$ Ō£ō (energy conserved).
Example 2 LR circuit

$\varepsilon_0=12$ V, $R=6$ ╬®, $L=3$ H; switch closed at $t=0$.

  1. $\tau=L/R=0.5$ s.
  2. $I(t)=2(1-e^{-2t})$ A; $I_\infty=2$ A.
  3. $U_{steady}=\tfrac12 LI^2=\mathbf{6.0\,J}$.
Example 3 Loop entering a field

Square side $a=0.10$ m, $B=0.30$ T, $v=2.0$ m/s, $R=0.50$ ╬®.

  1. While entering: $\varepsilon=Bav=\mathbf{0.060\,V}$, $I=\mathbf{0.12\,A}$.
  2. Once fully inside: $d\Phi/dt=0$, $I=0$.

Common Pitfalls

  1. Lenz's law: induced current opposes the change in flux, not flux itself.
  2. Inductor at $t=0^+$: open circuit ($I=0$). At steady state: a plain wire.
  3. $\Phi_B$ needs consistent $d\vec A$ orientation ŌĆö sets sign of $\varepsilon$.
Strategy

General Study Tips

How to prepare efficiently for both AP Physics C exams.

  1. Master calculus first. AP Physics C assumes fluent differentiation and integration. Nearly every unit has problems solvable only with calculus.
  2. Draw before solving. Free-body diagrams (Mechanics) and field-line/Gaussian-surface sketches (E&M) catch sign errors before algebra does.
  3. Parallel concepts across units. Rotational Ōåö translational (5/6); Gauss Ōåö Amp├©re (8/12); RC Ōåö LR (11/13); SHM Ōåö LC oscillation (7/13).
  4. Know the symmetry criterion. Gauss's and Amp├©re's laws are always true but only solvable when symmetry makes the field constant on the surface/loop.
  5. Practice old FRQs. CollegeBoard's past-exam archive is the single best resource.
  6. Units and sig figs. AP graders are lenient on sig figs but strict on units ŌĆö always check them.

What's NOT on the exam

Magnetic materials, AC circuits, displacement current, relativistic corrections, and quantum effects are not tested on AP Physics C. Focus energy on what's in the CED.
